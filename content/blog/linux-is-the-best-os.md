## Operating System (OS)

Most people don't even think about what their devices run on. Whether it's their mobile phone, laptop or desktop, they just use it with whatever's shipped with it. Most people use iOS, Android, Windows or macOS. But have you ever wonder what runs under the hood? 

Operating system, or OS for short, is what enable us to utilize these devices in our daily life. Each time ~we~ you turn on a laptop for example, ~we~ you would be logged in into Windows (I don't use Winblows). From there, you can open other apps that you want. Without Windows, it would be impossible to get to your apps. It can be thought of as the first layer in your computer system.

However, there are alternatives to Windows, and today we are going to take a dive into the Windows alternative, Linux. I brought five arguments on why Linux is the best OS in the whole wide world.

## Open Source

Linux is an open source software. If you want to know more about open source, you can Google or ask ChatGPT, Gemini, or whatever AI tools you’re into, to explain it to you, but I will give you an analogy as a physics graduate. 

We all know Isaac Newton right? He “discovered” gravity and do a lot of other cool stuff. Newton also introduce three law of motions that describe the relationship between the motion of an object and the forces acting on it. In this analogy, the law of motions are the “source”, and the fact that Newton decides to share with others, is the “open” part. If he did not decide to share that information with anyone, it would be similar with “proprietary” or “closed source”.

> "If I have seen further than others, it is by standing on the shoulders of giants" - Isaac Newton

Imagine if Newton did not share his finding with others, humanity would probably not be where we are now. The same thing could be said for software. Here are some statistics[^1] for mathematicians among us:
* 90% of Fortune 500 companies use open source software in production
* Linux powers 96.3% of the world's top 1 million supercomputers
* Open source saves companies $1.2 trillion annually in development costs

Essentially, open source acts as the fundamental base for the technology we use today. You can think of it as the bedrock that supports the entire digital world. 

## Performance & Resources

The most important factor to me when buying a new machine is the performance. I prefer something that is blazing fast so I can get my work done quickly and spend the time doing other useless things.

This is where Linux truly shines, especially if you're running on older or low-spec hardware. Unlike those other operating systems that get more bloated with every update (looking at you, Windows), Linux distros are lean, mean, and built for speed. Because you can choose a lightweight desktop environment and the OS only runs what's absolutely necessary, you save serious system resources. Less RAM being eaten up means more power for compiling code, running games, or just browsing thirty-seven tabs without your laptop sounding like a jet engine trying to take off. If you want a snappy, responsive machine, drop the resource hogs and go with Linux.

## Privacy & Freedom

Let's face it: in 2026, if a product is free, you are the product. Windows and macOS are constantly pinging servers, collecting telemetry data, and generally keeping tabs on what you’re doing. It’s hard to trust a closed-source system when you don't know exactly what's happening under the hood.

Linux, however, is a champion for user privacy. Since it’s open source, there are thousands of eyes constantly reviewing the code—if there was any shady data collection going on, someone would spot it immediately and call it out. With Linux, you get to decide what information (if any) leaves your machine. It’s the closest you can get to real digital sovereignty. You control your computer, your computer doesn't control you. That’s true freedom.


## Free (as in free)

One of the biggest arguments for making the switch is the zero-dollar price tag. You can download and install a full-featured, professional-grade operating system such as Ubuntu, Fedora, or Mint, without paying a single cent for the license.

Linux’s accessibility is exceptional. Even without a laptop, you can obtain an old laptop from platforms like Carousell or Facebook. Even decade-old hardware can run lightweight Linux distributions smoothly enough for web browsing, video streaming, or schoolwork, avoiding the lag of bloated modern software. This revitalizes "e-waste" into functional tools, proving that premium digital experiences don’t require high-end hardware.

Think about that. Windows 11 costs hundreds of dollars, and every new macOS machine requires buying expensive Apple hardware. With Linux, you get a premium experience, all the necessary software (like LibreOffice, which is basically free Microsoft Office), and security updates forever. For free. It’s not a trial version or a stripped-down community edition; it’s the whole damn thing, free of charge.


## Free (as in freedom)

This is the big one. As a physicist, I already touched on the importance of sharing knowledge with the open source argument, but 'free' in this context goes deeper than just the cost. It’s about the four essential freedoms guaranteed by the Free Software Foundation:

1. The freedom to run the program as you wish, for any purpose.
1. The freedom to study how the program works, and change it to make it do what you want (thanks to access to the source code).
1. The freedom to redistribute copies so you can help your neighbor.
1. The freedom to distribute copies of your modified versions to others.

Essentially, Linux is the ultimate DIY operating system. You have the permission and the tools to modify the OS, fix bugs, add features, and customize literally everything. This level of control is simply impossible with proprietary software like Windows or macOS. It ensures that the software always serves the user, not the corporation. That’s why Linux isn't just an alternative; it's the ethical choice for your computing life.

## Conclusion

So, there you have it. Whether it's the community-driven power of open source, the fact that it can breathe new life into an old laptop with its incredible performance, or the peace of mind you get from actual privacy, Linux is just in a league of its own. When you factor in that it costs absolutely nothing and grants you those four fundamental digital freedoms to truly own your software, the choice becomes pretty clear. Stop letting big tech companies dictate how you use your own hardware. It’s time to take the plunge, reclaim your digital life, and join the Linux revolution.
